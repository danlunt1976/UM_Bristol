# XTBG Paleoclimate Modelling Workshop 2026

## Guidance on exercise
The aim of the exercise is to address a research question of your own devising, related to paleoclimates.  To do this you will analyse model outputs from the University of Bristol Paleo Modelling Group. 

You will typically have about 3 days to complete your analysis (depending on how long it took you to work through the handbook), and then you will present your work in an EGU/AGU-style seminar, on the final afternoon of the workshop.

### Choosing a research question
* Deciding on a research question is often an iterative process.  You may start off with an initial question, but this may change as you explore the model outputs that you use to address the question, and perhaps find unexpected or interesting results that change your research direction.  
* In general there are two types of research question - (1) those that can be addressed solely using the model, for example exploring what mechanism is responsible for some model outputs, and (2) those that are related to model-data comparisons, for example asking how well the model reproduces some geologically-observed behaviour.  Often, the highest-profile work incorporates both of these, by first stating a hypothesis that has emerged from previous analysis of geological proxy data, then showing that the model can reproduce the signals seen in the geological record, and then using the model to test the hypothesis and explain the reasons why the model (and hopefully the real world) behaves that way.
* You could choose to focus on one particular time period, and understand that time period in detail, looking at many variables, or look across an ensemble at how a smaller number of variables change with time. 
* Once you have decided on your research question, think about how you will address is using the model outputs, and then discuss your plans with Dan before moving on.

### Visualisation and analysis
* It is always a good idea to start by visualising your model outputs.  As well as spatial patterns, explore global and/or regional means.  
* If you notice something interesting, then always aim to quantify it, rather than relying on your eye, which can be misleading.
* There are many types of in-depth analysis that can be applied, but in the time availabls it is unlikely that you will be able to get hugely deep into this analysis.  However, you can make a really good start by calculating pairs of quantities and regressing them against each other (see the handbook when we did this for model versus proxy temperatures, but you could extend this to, for example, regressing modelled winds strengths against, for example, meridional temperature gradients).  Some analysis tools that are useful in the longer term, which you may have time to apply if you are very rapid and ambitious, include energy balance analysis (e.g. Heinemann et al, 2009), forcing-feedback analysis (e.g. Farnsworth et al, 2019), and hydrological budget decomposition (e.g. Feng et al, 2022).   

### Possible avenues to explore

I don't want to be too prescriptive - it is nice if you are able to come up with your own ideas that interest you personally.  However, I realise that this can sometimes be daunting when you are new to paleo modelling, so here are just a few ideas to get you started, using either the Phanerozoic ensemble or the Plio-Pleistocene ensemble:
* How well do the Plio-Pleistocene simulations compare with proxy-derived estimates of global or local temperature? 
* What is the relationship between global mean temperature and precipitation through the Plio-Pleistocene, and how does this depend on ice cover?
* How do tropical Pacific longitudinal (zonal) temperature gradients link to ENSO variability through the Plio-Pleistocene?
* How does the barotropic (depth-averaged) ocean circulation vary through the Phanerozoic and why?
* How does climatic change control vegetation changes through the Phanerozoic?
* How do mid-latitude storms vary through the Phanerozoic and why?
* ... and many more possibilities - do ask me if you are unsure!

### Timetable

Different groups and individuals will work in different ways, but very broadly this is a guide to how you might choose to arrange your time over the three main days of analysis:

Wednesday:  You should identify all the simulations you need to answer your research question.  Focus today on really becoming familiar with your model outputs.  By the end of Wednesday, aim to have visualised all your relevant model data (for example as a series of maps), in a way that enables you to answer your research question.

Thursday: By the end of Thursday, aim to have analysed your model outputs to assess why the model gives the results that it does for example by carrying out correlation analysis, and/or carry out model-data comparisons

Friday: Spend the morning focussing on your key plots that you will include in your presentation, making them publication-quality, and writing your presentations.


**Good Luck!!**
